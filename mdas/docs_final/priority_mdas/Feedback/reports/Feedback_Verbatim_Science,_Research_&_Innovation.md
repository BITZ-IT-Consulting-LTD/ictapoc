### A1. Current Processes: Looking at the process maps presented, what parts are accurate and what may need correction or clarification?
Accurate: 
The process maps capture the key stages of research application, vetting, approval, and licensing, including submission, review, and authorization. Roles of responsible bodies are generally outlined.
Needs correction/clarification:
There is a need for clearer timelines, criteria for approval or rejection, and defined feedback mechanisms to applicants. The vetting process should be more detailed, especially on compliance, ethics, and quality checks. Additionally, the linkage between approval and licensing stages should be clarified to avoid duplication and ensure a smooth, transparent end-to-end process.

### A2. Current Processes (Completeness): Are there services, steps, or activities that were not included in the process maps?
Yes, there are a few services and activities that appear not to be fully captured in the current process maps. These include pre-application support such as guidance to applicants, stakeholder consultations, and awareness or sensitization activities. Additionally, some manual steps—such as internal reviews, document verification, and follow-ups—may not be well documented.
There is also a need to include post-service activities like monitoring, compliance checks, reporting, and feedback mechanisms from users. Integration with other agencies and systems, as well as customer support services, should be more clearly reflected to ensure a complete end-to-end process.

### B1. Proposed Processes: What thoughts do you have about how the proposed future processes may work in practice?
The proposed processes are well-structured and, if implemented effectively, can improve efficiency, transparency, and service delivery. However, their success will depend on the current capacity of the MDAs, including availability of skilled personnel, adequate funding, and supporting ICT infrastructure.
Some proposed changes may face operational constraints such as limited technical capacity, resistance to change, and reliance on manual processes that are not yet fully digitized. Integration with existing systems and coordination across departments may also present challenges.
To enhance practicality, there is a need for phased implementation, staff training, clear guidelines, and adequate resource allocation. Strengthening system interoperability and change management strategies will also be critical for successful adoption.

### B2. Proposed Improvements: What comments do you have about the proposed quick wins and priority improvements?
The proposed quick wins and priority improvements are generally practical and aligned with the goal of enhancing efficiency and service delivery. Quick wins are particularly valuable as they can deliver immediate, visible results with minimal resource requirements, helping to build momentum and stakeholder confidence in the reform process. They also provide an opportunity to test approaches, identify gaps early, and demonstrate the benefits of change.
However, their effectiveness will depend on how well they are prioritized and aligned with existing institutional capacities. Some quick wins may still face constraints such as limited staffing, inadequate funding, or weak ICT support, which could delay implementation or reduce their impact. There is also a risk that focusing too heavily on short-term gains may divert attention from more complex but critical long-term reforms.
Priority improvements, on the other hand, are essential for addressing systemic challenges and ensuring sustainable impact. These initiatives are likely to require more time, resources, and coordination across departments. As such, they must be supported by clear implementation plans, strong leadership, and effective monitoring and evaluation frameworks.
To strengthen both quick wins and priority improvements, it is important to ensure;
i) Realistic sequencing
ii) Adequate resource allocation
iii) Strong change management strategies
iv) Continuous stakeholder engagement
v) capacity building, and alignment with existing systems will also be key to ensuring successful and sustainable implementation.

### D2. Platform Integration: What considerations should be noted for integration with systems such as eCitizen, IPRS, or payment platforms?
Key integration considerations include:
i) Ensuring system interoperability
ii) Data security
iii) Secure user authentication. 
The platforms must support reliable transactions, high availability, and scalability, with proper logging for auditability. Seamless user experience, regulatory compliance, and robust change management are also essential for smooth integration.

### E1. Policy and Regulations: What policy or legal issues should be considered for implementation?
For implementation, key policy and legal issues to consider include:
i) Data protection and privacy: Compliance with national data protection laws when collecting, storing, and sharing personal and research data.
ii) Intellectual property rights: Clear rules on ownership, access, and use of research outputs stored in the national repository.
iii) Regulatory compliance: Alignment with existing laws and regulations governing research, payments, and digital services.
iv) Authentication and authorization: Legal recognition of digital approvals, signatures, and AI-based vetting decisions.
v) Cross-institutional agreements: Memoranda of understanding (MoUs) or legal frameworks for data sharing between MDAs, universities, and payment platforms.
vi) Accountability and liability: Clearly defined responsibilities in case of system errors, unauthorized access, or disputes over licenses.
vii) Records retention and archiving: Policies on how long research records and related data should be stored and the conditions for disposal.
viii) Cybersecurity obligations: Legal obligations for safeguarding systems against breaches, fraud, and misuse.
These considerations ensure that implementation is legally compliant, secure, and protects the rights of all stakeholders.

### E2. Institutional Arrangements: What comments do you have about the proposed governance structure, including the Digital Transformation Unit?
The proposed government structure, with a Digital Transformation Unit, provides centralized oversight, ensures strategic alignment, and supports capacity building and system integration. Its success depends on adequate staffing, technical expertise, funding, and effective change management to drive adoption and sustainable digital reforms.

### F1. Organisational Capacity: What skills gaps, training needs, or readiness issues should be noted?
Digital skills gaps include:
1) Limited ICT literacy 
2) Data management and analytics skills
3) System integration expertise
4) Cybersecurity awareness and understanding of AI and automation. 
Recommendation: Training programs should focus on basic digital skills, data handling, platform integration, cybersecurity, AI management, and change management for managers to ensure effective use and adoption of digital systems.

### F2. Leadership and Ownership: What observations do you have about leadership support and institutional ownership?
The MDA of Science, Research, and Innovation in Kenya operates under strong executive sponsorship provided by the Cabinet Secretary and Principal Secretary of Education, alongside the governing boards of key agencies such as NACOSTI, KeNIA, and NRF. This executive support ensures strategic alignment with national policies, resource allocation, and legitimacy for research and innovation initiatives, while championing transformation and removing bureaucratic obstacles. To sustain transformation, the MDA relies on a structured leadership framework comprising strategic leadership (Cabinet Secretary, Principal Secretary, and boards), operational leadership (directorates of research, innovation, and science policy), technical leadership (agency heads and project managers), and stakeholder engagement mechanisms (advisory committees, innovation hubs, and research networks). Critical principles underpinning this framework include clear governance, standardized processes with monitoring and evaluation systems, capacity building, and continuous stakeholder collaboration, all of which collectively ensure the effective promotion of science, research, and innovation in the country.
