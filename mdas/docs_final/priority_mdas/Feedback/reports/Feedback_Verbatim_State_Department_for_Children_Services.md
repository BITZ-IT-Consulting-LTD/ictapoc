Under Executive Summary
Adopt... 
The State Department is mandated to safeguard and strengthen families as the foundation of society while promoting the welfare, rights, and social development of children. This includes formulating and administering family protection policies, enforcing children’s right to parental care, and protecting them from abuse, neglect, harmful cultural practices, violence, exploitation, and other violations. It also ensures access to essential services such as nutrition, education, and psychosocial support through cross-sectoral collaboration with the Ministries of Health, Education, and Interior, as well as fostering early learning opportunities and inclusive programmes for children with disabilities.
In addition, the Department is tasked with regulating and supporting alternative care systems, rehabilitating street families, combating trafficking, and providing legal and rehabilitative services for children in conflict with the law. It further oversees childcare institutions, promotes strong family values through media regulation, and fosters partnerships with public, private, and community organizations to strengthen child welfare services. Through the administration of the Children Act, the National Programme for Orphans and Vulnerable Children, and international reporting obligations, the Department advances accountability and alignment with global child rights frameworks, ensuring that all children and families live in dignity and safety

Under Pain Points
-Replace with...

1. Fragmented Records: The coexistence of paper-based files and CPIMS leads to incomplete and non-synchronized case histories, undermining continuity of care, especially when children move across counties.
2. Delayed Response: Reliance on manual coordination and physical case review processes slows down timely intervention, particularly for high-risk and emergency cases.
3. Data Quality Issues: Double data entry (manual and digital) results in inconsistencies, duplication, and outdated information, weakening evidence-based decision-making.
4. Data Protection Risks: Mixed storage systems (physical and digital) with uneven safeguards expose sensitive child data to risks of loss, unauthorized access, and non-compliance.
5. Limited Interoperability: Weak integration between CPIMS and other sectoral systems (IPRS, health, education, police, judiciary) leads to inefficient referrals, poor tracking, and reduced accountability.

Under Opportunities (Strategic System Strengthening Areas)

3. Workflow Digitization: Introduce digital case conferencing and automated workflows to reduce delays, enhance responsiveness, and strengthen accountability through audit trails.
4. System Integration: Enhance interoperability with key institutions such as the Judiciary of Kenya and other sectors to enable end-to-end case management and coordinated service delivery.
5. Strengthened Data Protection: Improve both digital and manual data handling through role-based access, encryption, standardized protocols, and regular audits, ensuring full compliance with data protection requirements.

Future State Process (TO-BE)
Replace the Narrative to ...

The TO-BE process envisions a fully integrated, secure, and interoperable Child Protection Information Management System (CPIMS) that enables real-time, child-centred, and evidence-based case management across all counties.
This future state builds on the current hybrid system by progressively digitizing workflows, strengthening inter-agency coordination, and ensuring that all actions uphold the best interests of the child and compliance with the Data Protection Act 2019.

Under Core Components
-Replace with...

1. National CPIMS Platform 
CPIMS is the central digital case management system but is not yet fully deployed or consistently used nationwide.
It operates alongside paper systems, with gaps in connectivity, coverage, and user adoption limiting effectiveness.

2. Secure Case Records 
Case records are maintained in both physical and digital formats, creating a fragmented hybrid system.
While CPIMS has security features, inconsistent handling of physical files poses ongoing data protection risks.

3. Decision Support and Risk Assessment 
Risk assessment is mainly manual, guided by professional judgment and standard tools used by Children Officers.
CPIMS supports basic data capture, but advanced analytics like AI risk scoring are not yet in use.

4. Identity Verification 
Identification relies largely on manual processes, with limited integration into national systems like IPRS/Maisha Namba.
This creates challenges in tracking children, especially those without formal identification.

5. Judiciary Integration 
Seamlessly interfaces with the Judiciary Case Management System for rapid, digital filing of protection orders.
CPIMS supports limited tracking, but there is no real-time system integration.

6. Health System Linkages:
There is no direct integration with CPIMS, limiting verification of medical data and service access.
Links to national health registries (e.g., SHA) to verify medical histories and coverage for the child.
